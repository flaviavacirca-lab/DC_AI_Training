Draft Sharper Client Emails

Write polished, concise progress updates and follow-ups in one prompt.

The scenario: You need to send a progress update to a VP-level client sponsor at a Fortune 500 company. The email needs to be professional but warm, concise but complete, and clearly structured. Instead of staring at a blank email for 20 minutes, use Copilot to generate a strong first draft.

Weak vs. Improved Prompt

Weak Prompt
"Write an email to my client about the project."
You'll get something generic and vague. No specifics about findings, no clear next steps, wrong tone. You'll rewrite the whole thing.
Improved Prompt
"Draft a concise progress update email to a VP-level client sponsor at a Fortune 500 company. The project is a strategic growth assessment. Cover: (1) key findings so far — we've identified 3 potential market entry opportunities, (2) next steps — finalizing financial models next week, (3) any open items — we need their internal revenue data by Friday. Tone should be professional but warm, 150 words max."
You'll get a polished email with clear structure, the right tone for a senior audience, and all three key points covered concisely.

Why the improved prompt works

  • Audience: Specifying "VP-level" and "Fortune 500" sets the right formality and conciseness.
  • Content structure: Three numbered points (findings, next steps, open items) gives Copilot a clear framework.
  • Tone: "Professional but warm" prevents overly stiff or overly casual output.
  • Constraint: "150 words max" forces brevity, which senior clients appreciate.

Try This on Your Next Project

Draft a concise [EMAIL TYPE] to [RECIPIENT ROLE] at [COMPANY TYPE]. The project is [BRIEF CONTEXT]. Cover:
1. Key findings so far — [SUMMARY]
2. Next steps — [WHAT'S PLANNED]
3. Open items — [WHAT'S NEEDED FROM THEM]
Tone: professional but warm. Under [N] words.

Works great in Copilot in Outlook. You can also use this in the Copilot chat and paste the result.

Quick Workflow

1

Gather Context

Before prompting, identify: the recipient's role, the key message, the desired action, and any sensitivities. Two sentences of context beats a paragraph of generic instructions.

2

Draft with the Template

Use the template above in Copilot. For Outlook, use Copilot directly in the compose window. For standalone drafting, use the Copilot chat in Web mode.

3

Refine the Tone

Ask Copilot to adjust: "Make this more concise", "Soften the tone in paragraph 2", or "Add a specific call-to-action at the end." Iterate until it sounds like you.

Practice

Rewrite a Recent Email

Take a client email you sent last week. Paste it into Copilot and ask: "Improve this email for a VP audience. Make it more concise and add a clear next step." Compare the result to your original.

Tone Switching

Write one email update, then ask Copilot to rewrite it for three different audiences: your team lead, the client CEO, and a vendor. Notice how the structure, tone, and detail level change.

Common Mistakes

✗ Sending the AI draft without reading it through
✓ Always read the full email and adjust tone, facts, and next steps

Copilot drafts are good starting points but can include generic phrases or miss nuance. A quick read-through catches these.

✗ Asking for "a professional email" without specifying context
✓ Include the recipient's role, the situation, and the desired outcome

The more specific your context, the more the email sounds like something you'd actually send.
