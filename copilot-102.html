Copilot 102: Using Agents

Go beyond basic chat — learn how to use pre-built Copilot agents to automate workflows, get specialized outputs, and work faster across Microsoft 365.

What Are Copilot Agents?

Agents are specialized Copilot assistants that are pre-configured for specific tasks. Instead of starting from scratch every time and writing detailed prompts, agents come with built-in instructions, knowledge, and workflows tuned for a particular job.

Think of it this way: regular Copilot is a generalist — it can do many things if you tell it exactly what you want. An agent is a specialist — it already knows what you're likely trying to do and is set up to do it well.

Where to Find Agents

Agents are available in Microsoft 365 Copilot Chat (in Teams or the M365 app). Look for the right-side panel or the @ mention feature to browse and select available agents. Your organization may also have custom agents built in Copilot Studio.

Types of Agents

There are two categories of agents you'll encounter. Both work the same way — you just access them differently.

Microsoft Built-in Agents

These come pre-installed with your Microsoft 365 Copilot license. They're maintained by Microsoft and designed for common business tasks.

  • Available to everyone with a Copilot license
  • Regularly updated with new capabilities
  • Optimized for M365 data and workflows
  • No setup required — just find and use them

Custom / Organization Agents

These are built by your organization (or by you) using Microsoft Copilot Studio. They can be tailored to your firm's specific workflows and data.

  • Customized for your organization's needs
  • Can connect to internal data sources
  • Built and managed in Copilot Studio
  • Can be shared with specific teams or the whole org

Agents You Should Know

Here are the most useful agents for consulting work, organized by the type of task they help with.

What it does: Helps prepare for meetings and manage follow-up. It can create agendas, summarize past meetings on a topic, draft follow-up emails, and extract action items from transcripts.

Best for:

  • Preparing for a client check-in by reviewing past meeting notes
  • Generating a structured agenda based on outstanding items
  • Drafting follow-up emails with action items after a call
  • Tracking recurring discussion topics across multiple meetings

Example Prompt:

Review my meetings with the Acme Corp team over the last 2 weeks.
What are the open action items? Draft a follow-up email summarizing
what was discussed and what's still outstanding.

What it does: Searches across the web and your M365 data to find, compile, and synthesize information on a topic. It can pull from multiple sources and present a structured overview.

Best for:

  • Quickly building a landscape overview of an industry or topic
  • Finding internal documents related to a project
  • Compiling public information about competitors or markets
  • Synthesizing findings from multiple sources into a brief

Example Prompt:

Research the current state of the plant-based food market in North America.
Include market size, key players, recent M&A activity, and growth projections.
Synthesize into an executive-ready brief with sources cited.

What it does: Specializes in creating, editing, and formatting written content. It goes beyond basic Copilot in Word by offering more control over tone, structure, and style consistency.

Best for:

  • Drafting memos, proposals, or client-facing documents
  • Rewriting content for a different audience or tone
  • Editing for clarity, conciseness, and professional polish
  • Creating consistent document templates

Example Prompt:

Take the notes from last week's strategy session and draft a 2-page
client memo. Use a professional but accessible tone. Structure it as:
1. Executive Summary, 2. Key Findings, 3. Recommended Next Steps.

What it does: Focused on working with data — analyzing spreadsheets, identifying patterns, creating visualizations, and translating data into business insights.

Best for:

  • Analyzing survey results or financial data
  • Creating charts and graphs from raw data
  • Identifying trends, outliers, and correlations
  • Translating complex data into client-ready narratives

Example Prompt:

Analyze the attached revenue data for the last 8 quarters.
Identify the top 3 trends, flag any anomalies, and create a
line chart showing revenue by product category.
Summarize the story this data tells for the CEO.

What it does: Helps create and improve visual content — presentations, diagrams, infographics, and formatted layouts. It's particularly strong at turning text content into visual formats.

Best for:

  • Creating presentations from Word documents or meeting notes
  • Improving the visual design of existing slides
  • Generating diagrams and process flows
  • Formatting content for visual impact

Example Prompt:

Create a 12-slide board presentation from our strategy document.
Use a clean, professional layout. Include a title slide, executive
summary, one slide per strategic pillar (4 pillars), a timeline
slide, a financial summary, and a next steps slide.

What it does: Helps organize and track project work — extracting tasks from communications, creating status summaries, identifying blockers, and generating project updates.

Best for:

  • Extracting and organizing action items from multiple meetings
  • Creating weekly status reports from Teams activity
  • Identifying which workstreams are behind schedule
  • Drafting project update emails for stakeholders

channel this week. Create a status update that includes:
- Completed items

Which Agent for Which Task?

Use this quick-reference table to pick the right agent for common consulting scenarios.

Scenario Best Agent Why
Preparing for a client meeting Facilitator Reviews past meetings and creates agendas
Building an industry overview from scratch Researcher Searches and synthesizes information from multiple sources
Drafting a client memo or proposal Writer Optimized for structured, polished documents
Analyzing survey data or financials Interpreter Built for data analysis and visualization
Creating a board presentation Designer Focuses on visual output and slide design
Writing a weekly project status email Project Manager Aggregates updates from meetings, chats, and files
Summarizing a long email chain Copilot in Outlook No agent needed — built-in Outlook Copilot handles this well
Quick brainstorming or idea generation Copilot Chat (no agent) General-purpose Copilot is flexible enough for open-ended exploration

How to Use an Agent: Step by Step

1

Open Microsoft 365 Copilot Chat

Go to the Microsoft 365 app or open Copilot in Teams. This is the central hub where agents are accessible.

2

Browse or search for agents

Look for the agent selector in the right panel or type @ to see available agents. You can search by name or browse by category.

3

Select the agent and start your conversation

Click on the agent you want. It will open a focused chat with that agent's specialized capabilities. You can prompt it just like regular Copilot — but it already has context about its domain.

4

Iterate and refine

Just like regular Copilot, agents work best when you iterate. Ask follow-ups, request adjustments, and build on the output. The same chat-memory rules apply — stay in the conversation to maintain context.

Tips for Getting the Most from Agents

1

Don't Over-Prompt

Agents already have built-in instructions. You don't need to repeat their purpose — just tell them what you need. "Summarize last week's meetings" is enough for the Facilitator agent.

2

Use the Right Agent

A general-purpose Copilot prompt can often work, but the right agent gives you better output with less effort. Match the task to the specialist.

3

Combine Agents in Sequence

Use Researcher to gather information, then Writer to draft the deliverable, then Designer to build the presentation. Chain agents together for complex workflows.

4

Provide Specifics

Even though agents are specialized, they still benefit from specifics: client name, time range, file names, audience, and desired format.

When You Don't Need an Agent

Agents are powerful, but they're not always necessary. Here are situations where regular Copilot works just as well:

  • Quick one-off questions — "What's the definition of EBITDA?" or "Give me 5 icebreaker questions for a workshop."
  • Simple summarization in-app — Use Copilot directly in Outlook, Word, or Teams for straightforward summarization tasks.
  • Open-ended brainstorming — When you don't know exactly what you need yet, start with general Copilot and switch to an agent once you have direction.
  • One-time formatting tasks — "Convert these bullets into a numbered list" or "Rewrite this in a more formal tone."
