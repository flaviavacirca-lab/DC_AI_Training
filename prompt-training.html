Prompt Engineering for Consultants

Master the art of communicating with Microsoft Copilot to get high-quality, actionable outputs every time.

Why Prompting Matters

The quality of what you get from Copilot depends almost entirely on how you ask. A vague prompt gives vague results. A specific, well-structured prompt gives you something you can actually use — sometimes in seconds instead of hours.

Think of prompting as giving instructions to a very capable but very literal new analyst. They'll do exactly what you ask, so you need to ask clearly.

The CRAFT Framework

Use this five-part structure every time you prompt Copilot. The more elements you include, the better your result.

C

Context

Set the scene. What's the situation? Who's the audience? What background does Copilot need?

Example: "I'm a strategy consultant preparing a board presentation for a mid-market consumer packaged goods company."
R

Role

Tell Copilot what persona to adopt. This shapes the tone, depth, and perspective of the response.

Example: "Act as an experienced management consultant with expertise in CPG strategy."
A

Action

State clearly what you want Copilot to do. Use action verbs: analyze, summarize, draft, compare, list, outline.

Example: "Analyze the competitive landscape for premium pet food in the US market."
F

Format

Specify the output format: bullet points, table, executive summary, email, slide outline, etc.

Example: "Present this as a table with columns for competitor, market share estimate, key differentiators, and strategic positioning."
T

Tone & Constraints

Set boundaries: word count, tone (formal/casual), what to include or exclude, and any constraints.

Example: "Keep it under 300 words. Use a professional but accessible tone suitable for a C-suite audience. Do not include speculative revenue figures."

Prompt Patterns for Consulting Work

Template:

Summarize the following [document type] in [number] bullet points.
Focus on: [key themes or topics].
Audience: [who will read this].
Exclude: [anything irrelevant].

[Paste or reference the document]

Example:

Summarize this 40-page industry report in 8 bullet points.
Focus on: market size, growth drivers, and competitive dynamics.
Audience: A partner at a strategy consulting firm.
Exclude: methodology details and appendix data.

Template:

Draft a [tone] email to [recipient role] regarding [topic].
Key points to cover:
- [Point 1]
- [Point 2]
- [Point 3]
Keep it under [word count] words. End with [specific CTA].

Example:

Draft a professional but warm email to the VP of Strategy at a retail client
regarding the preliminary findings from our competitive analysis.
Key points to cover:
- We've identified 3 key strategic opportunities
- We'd like to schedule a working session next week to discuss
- Preliminary deck is attached for review
Keep it under 150 words. End with a question asking for their preferred meeting time.

Template:

Create a [number]-slide outline for a presentation on [topic].
Audience: [who].
Goal: [what should they walk away with].
For each slide, provide:
- Slide title
- 3-4 key bullet points
- Suggested visual or chart type

Example:

Create a 10-slide outline for a board presentation on digital transformation
opportunities in specialty retail.
Audience: CEO and Board of Directors of a $500M specialty retailer.
Goal: Secure approval for a $2M digital investment over 18 months.
For each slide, provide:
- Slide title
- 3-4 key bullet points
- Suggested visual or chart type

Template:

Act as a strategy consultant. Create a competitive analysis of [market/industry].
Include the following for each competitor:
- Company overview (1-2 sentences)
- Key strengths and weaknesses
- Market positioning
- Recent strategic moves

Present as a formatted table. Include [number] competitors.
Focus on the [geographic] market.

Template:

I have the following data: [paste data or describe dataset].
Analyze this and tell me:
1. What are the top 3 trends?
2. Are there any outliers or anomalies?
3. What story does this data tell for [audience]?
Present your analysis as [format].

Template:

I'm working on a strategy engagement for [client context].
The core question is: [strategic question].
Generate [number] hypotheses for why [observed trend or challenge].
For each hypothesis, provide:
- The hypothesis statement
- Supporting logic
- How we could test/validate it
- Potential data sources

Common Mistakes to Avoid

✗ "Summarize this."
✓ "Summarize this 40-page industry report in 8 bullet points, focused on market size and competitive dynamics, for a consulting partner."

Without context, Copilot doesn't know what matters to you.

✗ "Write an email to the client."
✓ "Draft a professional email to the VP of Strategy summarizing our Phase 1 findings. Include three key takeaways and a request to schedule a review meeting. Keep it under 150 words."

Specificity in audience, content, and format transforms the output.

✗ Accepting the first output as final.
✓ "Good start. Now make the tone more confident and add a specific data point from the analysis to support the second bullet."

Always iterate. The first response is a draft, not a deliverable.

✗ Asking for things Copilot can't verify.
✓ "Based on public information, what are the reported revenue ranges for these competitors? Flag any figures you're uncertain about."

Ask Copilot to flag uncertainty rather than trusting everything it generates.

Quick Reference: Power Phrases

            
Bookmark-worthy phrases to level up any prompt.

            

                

                    "Act as a [role] with expertise in [domain]"
                    Sets Copilot's perspective
                

                

                    "Present this as a [format] with [columns/sections]"
                    Controls output structure
                

                

                    "Keep it under [N] words/bullets"
                    Sets length constraints
                

                

                    "The audience is [role] who needs [outcome]"
                    Calibrates tone and depth
                

                

                    "Flag any assumptions or uncertain data"
                    Encourages transparency
                

                

                    "Compare [A] and [B] across [criteria]"
                    Structured comparison
                

                

                    "Generate [N] options, then recommend the best with rationale"
                    Gets exploration + recommendation
                

                

                    "First, outline your approach. Then execute."
                    Forces structured thinking
