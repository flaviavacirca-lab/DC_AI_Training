Competitive Analysis with Copilot Researcher

Build a structured competitor profile in 15 minutes using Microsoft 365 Copilot’s Researcher agent — no browser tabs, no manual Googling, just prompts and refinement.

When to Use This

Starting a new engagement

You need a quick landscape scan of 3–8 competitors before your first client meeting or internal kickoff.

Preparing a Phase 0 deck

You need competitor snapshots — revenue, positioning, recent moves — to populate an industry overview slide.

Building a strategy recommendation

You need to understand competitive dynamics (white space, differentiation, threats) to inform strategic options.

Responding to a client question

“What are our competitors doing in [area]?” — you need a defensible answer quickly.

Not the right module?

If you need to size a market (TAM/SAM/SOM, growth rates), see the Market Research Quick Win. If you need to synthesize interview transcripts, see Interview Synthesis.

What You’ll Get

By the end of this 15-minute workflow, you will have:

1

Competitor Profile Table

A structured table with HQ, revenue, employees, key products, target segments, and competitive advantages for each player.

2

Strategic Positioning Map

A recommended 2×2 positioning framework with suggested axes and where each competitor falls.

3

Recent Moves & Signals

A summary of acquisitions, product launches, partnerships, and leadership changes from the last 12 months.

4

White Space Analysis

Identified gaps, underserved segments, and opportunities where no competitor is dominant.

Tools & Setup

What You Need

  • Microsoft 365 Copilot — open via m365.cloud.microsoft/chat or the Copilot button in any M365 app
  • Web mode enabled — toggle to “Web” (not “Work”) so Copilot searches the public internet
  • Researcher agent — type @Researcher in the Copilot chat to invoke it (or use the agent picker)

Before You Start

  • Know which industry or market segment you’re researching
  • Have a rough list of 3–8 competitors (or let Copilot help you identify them)
  • Know your client’s positioning so you can frame the analysis from their perspective

Why Researcher?

The @Researcher agent is built for deep web research. Unlike standard Copilot chat, it follows multi-step search paths, cross-references sources, and produces longer, more structured outputs. It’s ideal for competitive intelligence gathering.

Step-by-Step Workflow

Follow these 5 steps in a single Copilot session. Total time: 10–20 minutes.

1

Identify the Competitive Set

Start with a broad scan to make sure you’re not missing key players. If you already have your competitor list, skip to Step 2.

Copy & paste this prompt:

@Researcher

I'm a strategy consultant researching the [INDUSTRY/SEGMENT] market
in [GEOGRAPHY].

Identify the top 8-10 competitors in this space. For each, provide:
- Company name and HQ location
- Estimated annual revenue (or revenue range)
- Primary products/services in this segment
- Target customer segment

Include both established leaders and notable challengers or disruptors.
Present as a numbered list. Flag any companies where the data is
estimated vs. confirmed from public filings.

What to look for: Scan the list for any missing players you know about. If Copilot missed one, reply: “Also include [Company X] — they recently entered this market via [acquisition/product launch].”

2

Build the Competitor Profile Table

Now go deeper on each competitor. This is the core deliverable most consulting decks need.

Copy & paste this prompt:

Now create a detailed competitor comparison table for these companies:
[LIST YOUR 5-8 COMPETITORS]

Columns:
| Company | HQ | Est. Revenue ($M) | Employees | Key Products/Services | Target Segment | Competitive Advantage | Recent Strategic Move (last 12 months) |

Requirements:
- Use the most recent publicly available data
- For private companies, provide revenue estimates with a range
- Flag any data points that are estimates vs. confirmed
- Sort by estimated revenue (largest first)
- After the table, list your sources

What to look for: Check that revenue figures pass a basic sanity test. If a number looks off, ask: “[Company X] revenue seems high/low — double-check against their latest annual report or press releases.”

3

Map Strategic Positioning

Create a 2×2 framework the team can put directly into a slide.

Copy & paste this prompt:

Based on the competitive data above, suggest a 2x2 positioning map
for this market.

Requirements:
1. Recommend the two best axes (explain why you chose them)
2. Place each competitor in a quadrant with a one-line rationale
3. Identify which quadrant represents the most attractive
   strategic position and why
4. Call out any "white space" quadrants where no competitor
   is strongly positioned

Format as a text-based 2x2 grid I can recreate in PowerPoint,
plus the rationale below.

What to look for: The suggested axes should be meaningful differentiators, not just “big vs. small.” Good axes examples: breadth vs. depth, enterprise vs. SMB, product-led vs. services-led, geographic focus vs. global.

4

Analyze Recent Moves & Signals

This is the “so what” layer — what competitors are actually doing matters more than their static profile.

Copy & paste this prompt:

For each of the competitors listed above, research their strategic
moves over the last 12 months:

For each company, find:
- Acquisitions or divestitures
- New product launches or major feature releases
- Key partnerships or alliances
- Leadership changes (CEO, CTO, board)
- Funding rounds or IPO activity
- Geographic expansion

Then provide:
1. A summary table: Company | Move | Date | Significance
2. Three "signal" insights: what do these moves collectively
   tell us about where this market is heading?
3. Any moves that directly threaten or create opportunity
   for [CLIENT NAME / your client's positioning]

What to look for: Copilot may miss very recent news (last 1–2 weeks). If you know of a recent move, add it: “Also note that [Company X] just announced [move] on [date]. Factor this into your analysis.”

5

Synthesize & Create the Deliverable

Pull everything together into a client-ready format.

Copy & paste this prompt:

Synthesize all the competitive analysis above into a structured
briefing for a consulting engagement.

Create:
1. Executive Summary (3-4 sentences: market overview, key dynamics,
   biggest competitive threats/opportunities)
2. Competitive Landscape Overview (1 paragraph)
3. The competitor comparison table (cleaned up)
4. The 2x2 positioning map with rationale
5. Top 5 strategic implications for [CLIENT CONTEXT]:
   - What does this mean for their competitive position?
   - Where are the biggest threats?
   - Where is the white space opportunity?
6. Recommended next steps for deeper analysis

Use an analytical, consulting tone. Lead with "so what" insights,
not just data. Format with clear headers I can paste into a slide
deck outline.

What to look for: The “strategic implications” section is where you add the most value. Use Copilot’s draft as a starting point, then add your own client-specific context and judgment.

Competitor Profile Template

Use this template as a starting point for individual deep-dive profiles. Copy the prompt below to generate a profile for any single competitor.

Single Competitor Deep-Dive Prompt:

@Researcher

Create a comprehensive competitor profile for [COMPANY NAME]
in the context of the [INDUSTRY/SEGMENT] market.

## Company Overview
- Full name, HQ, founding year, public/private status
- CEO and key leadership
- Mission/vision statement (if publicly stated)
- Estimated revenue, employee count, growth trajectory

## Products & Services
- Core offerings in this segment
- Pricing model (subscription, per-seat, usage-based, etc.)
- Key differentiators vs. alternatives
- Technology platform or approach

## Market Position
- Target customer segments (size, industry, geography)
- Estimated market share in [specific segment]
- Key wins or reference customers (publicly known)
- Channel strategy (direct, partners, self-serve)

## Strategy & Direction
- Recent strategic moves (last 12 months)
- Stated growth priorities (from earnings calls, press)
- Investment areas (R&D, geographic expansion, M&A)
- Potential vulnerabilities or challenges

## Competitive Assessment
- Top 3 strengths vs. [CLIENT or other competitors]
- Top 3 weaknesses or gaps
- Threat level to [CLIENT]: High / Medium / Low (with rationale)

Flag all estimates. Cite sources where possible.

Worked Example: B2B SaaS Customer Success Platforms

Here’s what a real workflow looks like, from first prompt to final output. Follow along or adapt the prompts for your own industry.

Prompt sent:

@Researcher
I'm researching the B2B customer success software market in North America.
Identify the top 8-10 competitors. For each: company name, HQ, estimated
revenue, primary product, target segment. Include leaders and challengers.

What Copilot returned: A list of 10 companies including Gainsight, Totango, ChurnZero, Planhat, Vitally, and others with estimated revenues ranging from $15M to $200M+.

What we did next: Added ClientSuccess and Catalyst (two players we knew from the client’s RFP process) by replying: “Also include ClientSuccess and Catalyst. They were in our client’s final evaluation.”

What Copilot produced: A 12-column table for 8 competitors. Three revenue figures were flagged as estimates.

Follow-up refinement:

Gainsight's revenue looks low. They raised $200M+ in funding and claim
1,000+ enterprise customers. Re-check and also add their recent Staircase AI
acquisition to the strategic moves column.

Lesson: Copilot corrected the estimate and added the acquisition. Always sanity-check the biggest players — that’s where errors are most visible to clients.

What Copilot suggested: Axes of “Enterprise vs. SMB focus” (x-axis) and “Platform breadth vs. Point solution” (y-axis).

Why this worked: These axes captured the real strategic tension in this market — some vendors are building all-in-one platforms while others specialize. The enterprise/SMB axis revealed different go-to-market strategies.

What we changed: Relabeled the x-axis from “Enterprise vs. SMB” to “Upmarket Enterprise vs. Mid-Market” to match how the client thinks about their market.

Total time: 18 minutes from first prompt to final synthesis.

What went into the client deck:

  • Competitive landscape overview slide (from the exec summary)
  • Competitor comparison table (reformatted in PowerPoint)
  • 2×2 positioning map (recreated as a visual)
  • Strategic implications slide with 5 “so what” bullets

What we added manually: Client-specific context (their positioning on the 2×2), our proprietary interview data, and a recommendation on which competitors to watch most closely. The AI did 70% of the research — we added the 30% of judgment and client context.

Practice Exercises

Try these on your own. Each takes 10–15 minutes. Use the workflow above and adapt the prompts.

Exercise 1: Your Current Engagement

Pick the client you’re working with right now. Run the full 5-step workflow for their competitive landscape. Compare the result to whatever competitive slides already exist in the engagement folder.

Goal: Find at least 2 competitors or data points the existing deck is missing.

Exercise 2: Adjacent Market Scan

A client asks: “Who would compete with us if we entered [adjacent market]?” Use Steps 1–3 to produce a competitive scan of the adjacent market and a positioning map showing where the client would likely land.

Goal: Produce a 1-page briefing in under 15 minutes.

Exercise 3: Disruption Watch

Use the “Recent Moves & Signals” prompt (Step 4) for an industry you follow. Then ask Copilot: “Which of these moves signals a potential disruption to the current market structure? Why?”

Goal: Identify one non-obvious insight about where the market is heading.

Exercise 4: Single Deep-Dive

Pick one competitor and use the Competitor Profile Template (Section E above). Then challenge Copilot: “What are the 3 biggest risks to this company’s market position over the next 2 years?”

Goal: Produce a profile you’d be comfortable sharing with a client.

Common Mistakes & Fixes

The Mistake

  • Trusting revenue numbers without checking. Copilot estimates revenue for private companies and sometimes gets it wrong by 2–3x.
  • Forgetting to use Web mode. In “Work” mode, Copilot searches your internal files, not the internet. You’ll get irrelevant results.
  • Asking for everything in one prompt. A single prompt like “give me a full competitive analysis” produces shallow, generic output.
  • Not specifying the geography or segment. “Competitors in SaaS” is too broad. Copilot needs a specific market definition.
  • Skipping the refinement step. The first output is a draft, not a deliverable. Always review, challenge, and ask follow-ups.
  • Putting AI-generated numbers in a deck without verification. A client will check. If Copilot says “$500M revenue” and the real number is $200M, your credibility is damaged.

The Fix

  • Cross-reference big numbers. For public companies, check investor relations pages. For private companies, use Pitchbook, Crunchbase, or note “estimated” explicitly.
  • Check the toggle before you start. The Web/Work toggle is at the top of the Copilot chat. Web = public internet. Work = your M365 files.
  • Use the 5-step workflow. Break the analysis into discrete prompts. Each step builds on the last and produces better output.
  • Define scope upfront. “B2B customer success software in North America for mid-market companies” gives Copilot a clear target.
  • Budget 5 minutes for refinement. After each step, ask 1–2 follow-up questions. This is where the quality jumps.
  • Label everything. Mark estimates as “~$XXM (est.)” in your deliverable. Clients respect transparency about data confidence.
