Market Mapping & Hypothesis Brainstorming

Create competitive landscapes and generate testable hypotheses in one prompt.

The scenario: You need to create a competitive landscape map for a client entering a new industry vertical. You want not just a list of competitors, but a structured map with positioning frameworks and hypotheses your team can test.

Weak vs. Improved Prompt

Weak Prompt
"List competitors in fintech."
You'll get a flat list with no segmentation, no positioning, and no strategic framing. Not useful for a client deck.
Improved Prompt
"I'm mapping the competitive landscape for a client entering B2B fintech payments. Help me create a structured competitive map that includes: (1) 15–20 key players categorized by segment (processing, infrastructure, embedded finance), (2) for each: estimated revenue range, funding stage, key differentiator, (3) a 2x2 framework suggestion for positioning these players (e.g., scale vs. specialization), and (4) 3 hypotheses about where the market is headed. Format as a table I can paste into a deck."
You'll get a segmented competitive map, a positioning framework, and forward-looking hypotheses — all structured for a client presentation.

Why the improved prompt works

  • Segmentation: Categorizing by segment creates structure, not just a list.
  • Depth: Revenue, funding, and differentiators give each entry analytical value.
  • Frameworks: The 2x2 positioning request creates a strategic lens.
  • Forward-looking: Hypotheses push beyond descriptive analysis into strategic insight.

Try This on Your Next Project

I'm mapping the competitive landscape for a client in [INDUSTRY/VERTICAL]. Help me create:
1. [N] key players categorized by segment ([LIST SEGMENTS])
2. For each: estimated revenue range, funding stage, key differentiator
3. A 2x2 positioning framework (e.g., [AXIS 1] vs. [AXIS 2])
4. 3 hypotheses about where the market is headed
Format as a table I can paste into a deck.

The 2x2 framework is especially useful for the "competitive landscape" slide. Verify company details against primary sources.

Human Review Required

Before using competitive maps, verify that company descriptions are accurate, revenue estimates are reasonable, and positioning reflects real market dynamics.

Quick Workflow

1

Define the Map Dimensions

Decide what you're mapping: competitors, market segments, or strategic options. Choose 2 meaningful axes (not just "big vs. small" — think about what actually differentiates players).

2

Generate the Landscape

Use the template prompt above. Copilot will suggest players and positioning. Review critically — does the placement match your understanding of the market?

3

Generate Hypotheses

Follow up: "Based on this competitive map, what are 5 testable hypotheses about where the market is heading?" Use these as a starting point for your engagement's hypothesis set.

Practice

Map Your Client's Market

Run the template for a client's competitive landscape. Then ask Copilot to suggest the best 2×2 axes and place each competitor. Does the visual tell a clear story?

Hypothesis Generation Sprint

After generating a map, ask Copilot for 10 hypotheses. Rate each: testable (yes/no), interesting (1-5), evidence available (easy/hard). Pick the top 3 for your engagement.

Common Mistakes

✗ Using generic axes like "price vs. quality"
✓ Choose axes that reflect real strategic tensions in the market

Good axes reveal insight. Examples: "product-led vs. services-led", "enterprise vs. mid-market", "platform breadth vs. point solution".

✗ Treating the AI's positioning as definitive
✓ Validate placement against your primary research and client knowledge

Copilot may misplace companies it has less data on. Cross-reference with your engagement team's knowledge.
